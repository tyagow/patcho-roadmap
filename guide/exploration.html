Typography

A three-role system (display · body · accent) across four moods. Every primary pick is Google-Fonts-loadable and free for commercial use (SIL OFL 1.1, except Permanent Marker = Apache 2.0) — so Patcho ships web, app, and printed garments from one open-source stack with zero licensing invoices. Commercial faces (Druk, Knockout, United, Tungsten) are named for reference only. All specimens below render in their actual fonts.

Mood 1 · Grunge / Distressed — Thrasher, Santa Cruz

Rebellion, DIY, xeroxed-flyer authenticity. Thrasher's masthead is Banco (Excoffon, 1951) — commercial and trademark-adjacent, so do not clone it. The skate-grunge signature comes from distressing a strong display face in-layer, not from a sketchy "free grunge font." Clean-licensed blocky options below.

Logo Archetypes

A logo system, not one mark: a primary device, a compact icon for small spaces, and an art direction that survives a huge back panel and a tiny sleeve cuff. In this culture the logo isn't a corporate signature — it's a tribal marker you fly to show you belong. Five archetypes, rated for a dirt brand. The name "Patcho" hands us a once-in-a-brand gift: the device and the name are the same word.

ABold Wordmark / Logotype
MerchA+ScaleA+DistinctC+

The dominant archetype in action sports — apparel is the canvas. Craft lives in one signature letterform "trick": Vans' roof-"V", Thrasher's removable flame skin (clean for editorial, burning for merch), Fox's tonal "Wordmark" line.

  • Legible at any size, any garment
  • Cheapest path to recognition
  • Endlessly remixable (Thrasher lesson)
  • Hardest to make distinctive
  • No standalone stamp
  • Gives no collectible object
BBadge / Emblem / Crest
MerchAScaleBDistinctB+

A bounded shape bundling name, year, location, motif — reads as heritage and belonging. Not coincidentally the native shape of a patch. Santa Cruz red dot, Independent crest, Yeti's ownable turquoise lockup.

  • Instant "club" feeling — perfect for a yard/crew
  • A shape that's inherently a patch/pin/sticker
  • Holds tagline + est. date
  • Detail-dense badges shrink poorly
  • Can drift generic "craft-brewery"
  • Slower to read in motion/thumbnails
CMascot / Character
MerchA+ScaleBDistinctA

Often the most-loved and most-merched asset a brand owns. Santa Cruz's Screaming Hand started as a wheel graphic and outgrew the logo — the canonical lesson. Best held as a second-act asset layered on the wordmark.

  • Highest emotional ceiling — soul-brand fuel
  • Spawns infinite tees, poses, collabs
  • Distinctive by default
  • Expensive, risky to get right
  • Style-locked, can date
  • Not a clean utility mark alone
DMonogram / Icon
MerchB+ScaleA+DistinctC→A

The favicon / headbadge / embossing workhorse. HUF's "H" is the hat; Chris King's restraint signals precision; component brands (Deity, Spank, DMR) etch icon-marks on small machined parts.

  • Ultimate scalability (favicon → headbadge)
  • "Hardware-credible" — signals you make parts
  • Cheap, consistent everywhere
  • Low meaning early (must be taught)
  • Coldest for a soul brand alone
  • Single letters collide easily
EPatch-Based System Hero for Patcho
MerchA++ScaleB+DistinctA++

Here the medium is the identity: the core device isn't a flat logo, it's a physical patch and the system of patches — bounded, tactile, collectible objects you attach, swap, trade, and earn. The brand becomes a patch label the way Supreme is a box-logo label. For a brand literally named Patcho, the name, device, and product become one closed loop — no competitor can out-authenticate it.

The heritage kit

Military → morale-patch lineage (official insignia identifies the unit; the morale patch captures what the unit actually was — "official enough to identify, personal enough to mean something"). The mid-2000s Velcro shift turned the patch from a permanent statement into a swappable, rotating identity signal — that one mechanical change built the modern morale-patch industry. Plus scout sashes, MC back-patch "colors," and streetwear patch craft (embroidered, 3D puff, chain-stitch, woven, leather, PVC, sublimated).

How Patcho makes the patch its core device

  • One hero patch = the brand stamp (badge-shaped, embroidered/chain-stitch, merrow-edge) — the box-logo equivalent.
  • Velcro-backed swap system as the signature mechanic — garments ship with a hook field; patches drop/earn/collab separately. You Patcho your gear.
  • A patch language: core logo + numbered crew patches + earned patches (sessions, trips, the home yard) + collabs, in 50–100 unit batches → scarcity + belonging, tradeable currency.
  • Placement grammar (identity front-left chest, rotating/joke patch on back/sleeve) — a culture with rules you opt into.
  • Design for artifact status — distressing/fraying become features; the product literally ages better in the dirt ("Built on dirt").
Recommendation Lead with the patch system (E) as the core device, anchored by a bold custom wordmark (A) inside a badge-shaped (B) hero patch, with a monogram (D) fallback for favicons/labels and a mascot (C) held in reserve as a second-act asset. The patch contains all archetypes as needed while making the name, device, product, and community ritual a single self-reinforcing loop.

Merch & Application

The real merch landscape Patcho is entering — grounded in what Fasthouse, DHaRCO, Loose Riders, TLD, Fox, Yeti, Thrasher and Santa Cruz actually ship in 2025-2026. Strategic core: the tee, sticker, patch and cap ARE the brand at launch; the jersey is a graduation, not a starting point.

The Format Stack

Core · Launch

Graphic tee

The anchor and primary canvas — where the soul lives. Heavyweight cotton, boxy fit. Everything orbits the tee.

Core · Launch

Stickers

Cheapest brand artifact, best conversion tool. The $5 sticker converts the rider who hesitated on the $40 tee — and spreads at the trailhead.

Core · Launch

Patches

Read as authentic, DIY, collectible. Iron-on / velcro. Sit perfectly with "Born in the yard." Low MOQ.

Core · Launch

Caps

Second-biggest identity item. Trucker (hero) + corduroy rope hat in drop one.

Core · Launch

Hoodie / crew

Higher ticket, higher margin, big back-print canvas. One or two colorways, not a range.

Phase 2

Beanies · socks · flannel · coaches jacket

Cheap on-brand adds + rugged-workwear crossover (TLD Ruckus, LR flannels) once cashflow + audience exist.

Phase 3 · Capital

DJ / MTB jersey

The genre's signature performance piece — but needs technical fabrics + real pattern work. Earn the right to make it on cotton first.

2025-2026 Graphic & Garment Trends

Silhouette & blank

  • Structured oversized/boxy is the dominant cut — now an expectation
  • Heavyweight cotton (220-260+ gsm) that holds shape = trust signal; collapsing necklines kill repeat orders
  • Texture (slub, waffle, rib) as perceived premium

Washes & dye

  • Garment / pigment-dyed — broken-in feel (Fasthouse "Render")
  • Acid / mineral / crystal wash — Fasthouse literally sells "Crystal Wash"; reads anti-corporate
  • Vintage/faded = "earned, not bought"

Decoration (match technique to story)

  • Screen print — genre standard, cheapest at volume
  • Puff & crackle ink — cheap elevation, signature streetwear move
  • DTG / DTF — no minimums; test artwork before committing
  • Embroidery / 3D puff — gold standard for caps/hoodies; flat cost = friendly for small runs

Placement vocabulary

  • Front badge + full back (hero/storytelling) + sleeve hit + nape + hem — multi-placement rewards a closer look. Front-and-back is now baseline.

What Makes a Tee Actually Sell

The Thrasher case is canonical: the tee began as a free magazine giveaway, got so coveted fans resold it, then became a product. Credibility first, commerce second.

  • Earned authenticity over manufactured cool — the scene polices posers. Patcho's Brazilian founders + real trail/foam-pit footage is the credibility engine.
  • Insider references — specific trails, tricks, slang turn a tee into a tribal marker.
  • Riders/athletes as the story — a small roster in real footage beats any ad spend.
  • Story over logo-slap — back-print storytelling outperforms a chest logo.
  • Drops & scarcity — numbered runs, email-first access; sell-outs are marketing.
  • Quality that earns repeat wear — 2026 buyers reward fit + shape-retention; the second purchase is where soul brands compound.
  • Off-the-bike wearability — the casual line is the moat (Fasthouse, Loose Riders won by being everywhere, not just on riders).
Net implication Open with a tight high-identity core: 1-2 heavyweight boxy tees (front badge + storytelling back print, a wash colorway), a sticker pack, a patch, a puff-embroidered trucker, a corduroy rope hat. Test artwork with DTG/DTF, screen-print the winners (puff/crackle upgrades), embroider headwear. Release as scarce, numbered, email-first drops tied to real footage and riders. Defer jerseys/socks/workwear.

Texture & Motif Library

Patcho's visual vocabulary — a kit of recurring motifs rooted in the literal materials and rituals of backyard dirt-jumping. The discipline lesson from the best brands: own a small set of hero devices and apply them relentlessly (Fox built an empire on one angular head). Ten motif families feed three layers — hero marks, workhorse badges/patches, and texture overlays.

Signature Ownable Hero Devices

From the families above, the few marks worth investing in as the recognizable Patcho — the ones for the hangtag, the woven patch, and (done right) people's skin:

1

The Lip

A single confident line-art curve abstracted from a dirt-jump takeoff lip — doubles as a check-mark and a smile, works at any scale, the most logo-able shape in the sport. Patcho's "swoosh." Add a dotted air-arc for the full "send" lockup. Primary brand-mark candidate.

2

The Patch

Lean into the name: an ownable, irregular stitched-edge patch silhouette (torn-and-restitched shield-blob) as the universal container for every emblem — recognizable even empty. Triple meaning: a sew-on patch, a patch of dirt, patching the trail. The most natural equity grab available. Primary system device.

3

The Foam-Cube Monogram

A blocky "P" built from isometric stacked foam cubes — distinctive, unexpected, directly tied to Patcho's actual foam pit. Almost nobody in dirt apparel owns this. Doubles as a color-system device (each colorway = a cube color). Secondary mark / merch hero.

4

The Crossed-Shovels "No Dig No Ride" Crest

Two crossed spades + est. year — the "Yard Built" dig-crew insignia. The soul mark for the dig-culture faithful and the natural centerpiece of a premium/heritage drop. Workhorse badge.

System logic The Patch is the universal frame → it holds The Lip, the Cube Monogram, or the Shovel Crest depending on the piece. Hero apparel carries a topo "yard map" or jungle back-print. Everything is finished with ONE house halftone-grunge distress, and the stencil treatment when DIY energy is wanted. Palette: dirt browns/tans + jungle greens base, rotating foam-cube accent per season. Two type registers: angular moto (gnarly line) + soft inflated-resi forms (playful/progression line). A natural capsule arc falls right out: a "Foam → Resi → Dirt" three-drop progression telling the brand's own learning story.
